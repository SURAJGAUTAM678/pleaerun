India's democracy promised to be a great equalizer — a system where every citizen, regardless of caste, class, or creed, would have an equal voice. Yet, critics argue that under the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), the corridors of power have increasingly echoed the interests of India's corporate elite over its vast working-class majority. Nowhere is this more visible than in the lives of ordinary workers — skilled, hardworking people — who work the same jobs as permanent employees but live under the constant shadow of expiry, thanks to a labour arrangement called Fixed Term Employment.

The Concentration of Wealth

Since 2014, India has witnessed a dramatic widening of its wealth gap. According to the World Inequality Lab's landmark 2024 study by Thomas Piketty and colleagues — titled "The Rise of the Billionaire Raj" — India's top 1% now controls 40.1% of the nation's total wealth and 22.6% of national income. These are the highest levels recorded since British rule.

Meanwhile, the bottom 50% of Indians — over 700 million people — share just 6.5% of national wealth. The 10,000 wealthiest individuals in India own an average of ₹22.6 billion each — that is 16,763 times the national average. The report's authors warn starkly of "India's slide toward plutocracy."

The FTE Trap: Same Work, No Security

Among the most telling symbols of BJP's labour philosophy is the dramatic expansion of Fixed Term Employment. Before 2018, FTE was restricted only to the garment and textile sectors. The BJP government extended it to all sectors through the Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Amendment — a gift to employers wrapped in the language of "flexibility" and "ease of doing business."

For workers, however, FTE means something far grimmer. Across industries — manufacturing, power, infrastructure, services — Fixed Term Employees work alongside permanent staff, doing the same work, putting in the same hours, carrying the same responsibilities. Yet they receive:

⛔No permanency, regardless of years of service or performance
🔄No assurance of renewal — living in perpetual anxiety
📉Reduced access to service benefits vs permanent staff
🤐Culture of silence — speak up and lose your contract
🚫No real union protection in practice
📋Renewal entirely on employer's terms — no ladder, only a treadmill

This is the modern face of labour insecurity in India — not the informal daily wage worker on a construction site, but the skilled, qualified professional who has given years of productive service and still cannot call his job his own.

Who Benefits From FTE?

The answer is not the worker. By replacing permanent positions with fixed-term contracts, companies dramatically reduce their long-term wage bills, pension liabilities, gratuity obligations, and union commitments. The financial savings flow upward to shareholders and management. The insecurity flows downward to the worker.

Critics argue this is entirely consistent with a broader BJP economic philosophy: reduce the cost of labour, increase the return on capital. The FTE model is not a coincidence — it is a design.

"The FTE model gives employers all the benefits of a permanent workforce — reliability, skill, institutional knowledge — without any of the long-term obligations."

Labour Laws: Tilting the Balance

The BJP government's consolidation of 44 labour laws into four labour codes has been lauded by industry but deeply criticized by trade unions across the country. These codes dilute workers' rights to organize, reduce job security, and make it structurally easier for employers to hire and fire at will.

For an FTE worker, every contract renewal is a negotiation conducted entirely on the employer's terms. There is no ladder to climb. There is only a treadmill to stay on — and pray it doesn't stop.

The Cruelest Irony: Public Sector FTEs

If FTE in private industry is troubling, FTE in the public sector is a profound betrayal. Across several government-owned companies and public sector undertakings — profitable enterprises built on taxpayer money, posting thousands of crores in annual profits — workers are being engaged on fixed-term contracts rather than given permanent employment.

These are not struggling companies trying to survive. These are entities that can afford permanency but choose not to offer it. This is a political choice, not an economic compulsion. When a profit-making public enterprise denies job security to its own workforce — it is not a policy failure. It is the policy succeeding exactly as designed.

Electoral Funding and the Bond Question

The Electoral Bond scheme, struck down by the Supreme Court in 2024 as unconstitutional, laid bare the transactional nature of the relationship between big business and the BJP. Companies that received government contracts, regulatory approvals, or relief from investigative agencies purchased electoral bonds — the overwhelming majority of which flowed to the BJP.

Meanwhile, an FTE worker somewhere in India — skilled, essential, working full time — wonders if there is anyone in the halls of power thinking about him.

Crony Capitalism and the Bigger Picture

The FTE expansion fits into a broader, consistent pattern. The exponential growth of select corporate conglomerates, the devastation of the informal economy through demonetisation, the rushed GST rollout that crushed small businesses, the farm laws that sought to open agricultural markets to large corporations — all tell the same story.

In every domain — agriculture, industry, public enterprise, financial services — the BJP's policy architecture tilts decisively toward those with capital and away from those who have only their labour to offer.

The Other Side of the Argument

Supporters of BJP argue that FTE actually formalises workers who were previously employed informally with no contracts, no PF, and no ESI coverage. They point to economic growth, infrastructure investment, and welfare schemes like PM Awas Yojana, Jan Dhan, and Ujjwala that have materially improved the lives of millions. They contend that pro-business policies generate the jobs and tax revenue that fund social spending. The government also notes that unemployment fell from 6% in 2018 to 3.2% in 2023, and female workforce participation has risen significantly over the past decade.

Conclusion: Whose India Is It?

The Fixed Term Employee is not a marginal figure. He or she is a skilled, productive, taxpaying citizen — essential to India's growth story — who has been systematically denied the dignity of job security by a policy framework designed to serve employers, not workers.

When the top 1% hold 40% of national wealth, when 185 billionaires have seen their collective wealth surge 42% in a single year, when labour protections are dismantled code by code, when public sector workers are denied permanency — the burden of proof lies with the government to demonstrate that its vision of India includes the worker, not just the owner.

As Abraham Lincoln once defined democracy as "of the people, by the people, for the people" — millions of Fixed Term Employees across India are today asking one simple, urgent question:

Whose people?
